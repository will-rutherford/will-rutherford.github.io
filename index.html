🧠 Must-Memorise — NOT on the formula sheet

The formula sheet only gives you: trig identities, derivatives (incl. arcsin/arccos/arctan), volume of revolution $V=\pi\int_a^b[f(x)]^2dx$, the geometric-series sum, and the Taylor-series template. Everything below you must know cold — they are not provided and are needed constantly.

Antiderivatives (no integral table given!)

$\int x^a dx=\dfrac{x^{a+1}}{a+1}+C\ (a\ne-1)$
$\int \dfrac1x dx=\ln|x|+C$
$\int e^x dx=e^x+C,\quad \int a^x dx=\dfrac{a^x}{\ln a}+C$
$\int \sin x\,dx=-\cos x+C$
$\int \cos x\,dx=\sin x+C$
$\int \sec^2x\,dx=\tan x+C$
$\int \dfrac{1}{1+x^2}dx=\arctan x+C$
$\int \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}dx=\arcsin x+C$

Integration techniques

By parts: $\int u\,dv=uv-\int v\,du$. (LIATE: pick $u$ = Log, Inverse-trig, Algebraic, Trig, Exp — in that priority.)

u-sub: spot inner function $g$ whose derivative $g'$ multiplies the rest.

Partial fractions: $\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}$. Cover-up to get $A,B$.

$\int\frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}dx=\ln|f(x)|+C$ — e.g. $\int\frac{2x}{x^2+3}dx=\ln(x^2+3)+C$.

Convergence tests (NOT given)

nth-term (divergence): if $a_n\not\to0$, diverges. (Can NEVER prove convergence.)

Geometric: $\sum ar^n$ converges $\iff|r|<1$, to $\frac{a}{1-r}$.

p-series: $\sum\frac1{n^p}$ converges $\iff p>1$.

Ratio test: $L=\lim|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}|$; $L<1$ conv, $L>1$ div, $L=1$ inconclusive.

Comparison: $0\le a_n\le b_n$; $\sum b_n$ conv ⇒ $\sum a_n$ conv.

Alternating (Leibniz): $\sum(-1)^n b_n$ converges if $b_n\downarrow0$.

Absolute vs conditional

Absolutely convergent: $\sum|a_n|$ converges.

Conditionally convergent: $\sum a_n$ converges but $\sum|a_n|$ diverges (e.g. alternating harmonic $\sum\frac{(-1)^n}{n}$).

$\sum\frac{(-1)^n}{n}$ converges (alternating) but NOT absolutely — classic answer.

Key Maclaurin series (know these 3)

$e^x=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\dfrac{x^n}{n!}=1+x+\dfrac{x^2}{2!}+\cdots$
$\cos x=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\dfrac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!}=1-\dfrac{x^2}{2!}+\dfrac{x^4}{4!}-\cdots$
$\sin x=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\dfrac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}$

All three converge for ALL real $x$ ($R=\infty$). Coefficient of $x^2$ in $\cos x$ is $-\tfrac12$.

Linear algebra facts (NOT given)

2×2 inverse: $A^{-1}=\frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{bmatrix}d&-b\\-c&a\end{bmatrix}$.

$\det(AB)=\det A\det B$; $\det(A^{-1})=\frac{1}{\det A}$; $\det(A^T)=\det A$.

$\det(cA)=c^n\det A$ for $n\times n$ (so $\det(2A)=4\det A$ for 2×2).

$A$ invertible $\iff\det A\ne0\iff$ null space $=\{0\}$ $\iff$ columns lin. indep.

Eigen: solve $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$ for $\lambda$; eigenvector = null space of $(A-\lambda I)$.

Triangular matrix: eigenvalues = diagonal entries (also $\det$ = product of diagonal).

Ch 7–8 · Series, Power Series & Taylor

Part A — series convergence (the decision flowchart)

  1. Does $a_n\to0$? No ⇒ diverges by nth-term test. Done.
  2. Geometric ($ar^n$)? Use $|r|<1$.
  3. p-series ($\frac1{n^p}$)? $p>1$ converges.
  4. Alternating ($(-1)^n b_n$)? Leibniz: $b_n\downarrow0$ ⇒ converges.
  5. Factorials / $n$th powers? Ratio test.
  6. Looks like a known series? Comparison / limit comparison.
  7. Telescoping? Write partial sum, watch it collapse.
The nth-term test can only prove divergence. $a_n\to0$ does NOT prove convergence (harmonic $\sum\frac1n$ diverges though $\frac1n\to0$).
Worked: comparison test — show $\sum\frac{1}{n^3+n}$ converges

For $n\ge1$, $\dfrac{1}{n^3+n}\le\dfrac{1}{n^3}$. Since $\sum\frac{1}{n^3}$ is a p-series with $p=3>1$, it converges. By the comparison test, $\sum\frac{1}{n^3+n}$ converges. ∎

Worked: classify $\sum\frac{(-1)^n}{n}$

Alternating, $b_n=\frac1n\downarrow0$ ⇒ converges (Leibniz). But $\sum|a_n|=\sum\frac1n$ is the harmonic series, which diverges. So it is conditionally convergent.

⭐ Part B — Radius & Interval of Convergence

For $\sum c_n(x-a)^n$:

  1. Apply the ratio test to $|c_{n+1}(x-a)^{n+1}/c_n(x-a)^n|$. Take $n\to\infty$.
  2. Set that limit $<1$ ⇒ get $|x-a|radius of convergence.
  3. Interval is centred at $a$: $(a-R,a+R)$. Now test each endpoint separately by substituting $x=a\pm R$ and checking the resulting number series.
  4. Write the final interval with correct open/closed brackets.
Worked: $\sum\frac{2^n}{n\,3^n}(x-1)^n$ — radius & interval

Ratio: $\left|\frac{2^{n+1}(x-1)^{n+1}}{(n+1)3^{n+1}}\cdot\frac{n3^n}{2^n(x-1)^n}\right|=\frac{2}{3}\cdot\frac{n}{n+1}|x-1|\to\frac23|x-1|$.

Converges when $\frac23|x-1|<1\Rightarrow|x-1|<\frac32$, so $R=\frac32$. Centre $x=1$ ⇒ interval $(-\frac12,\frac52)$ before endpoints.

$x=\frac52$: term $=\frac1n$, $\sum\frac1n$ diverges. $x=-\frac12$: term $=\frac{(-1)^n}{n}$, alternating harmonic — converges.

Interval of convergence: $\boxed{[-\tfrac12,\ \tfrac52)}$.

⭐ Part B — Taylor series from the definition

  1. Compute $f(a),f'(a),f''(a),\dots$ until you see the pattern.
  2. Plug into $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n$ (this template IS on the sheet).
  3. If asked for radius, ratio-test the result.
Worked: Taylor series for $f(x)=e^x$ about $x=1$ + radius

$f^{(n)}(x)=e^x$ so $f^{(n)}(1)=e$ for all $n$. Hence $e^x=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{e}{n!}(x-1)^n$.

Radius: ratio $=\left|\frac{(x-1)^{n+1}/(n+1)!}{(x-1)^n/n!}\right|=\frac{|x-1|}{n+1}\to0<1$ for all $x$, so $R=\infty$.

Reuse trick (part b): $g(x)=(x-1)e^x=(x-1)\sum\frac{e}{n!}(x-1)^n=\sum\frac{e}{n!}(x-1)^{n+1}$ — just multiply the known series.

Worked: $\int\frac{1}{x(x+1)}dx$ (partial fractions)

$\frac{1}{x(x+1)}=\frac{1}{x}-\frac{1}{x+1}$. Integrate: $\ln|x|-\ln|x+1|+C=\ln\left|\frac{x}{x+1}\right|+C$.

Worked: $\int \ln x\,dx$ (by parts, the sneaky one)

Take $u=\ln x,\ dv=dx$ ⇒ $du=\frac1x dx,\ v=x$. Then $\int\ln x\,dx=x\ln x-\int x\cdot\frac1x dx=x\ln x-x+C$.

⭐ Part B — Improper integrals

  1. Replace the bad limit ($\infty$ or a singularity) with a variable, e.g. $\int_1^\infty=\lim_{R\to\infty}\int_1^R$.
  2. Integrate normally (often partial fractions first).
  3. Take the limit. Finite ⇒ converges (give the value). $\pm\infty$ or DNE ⇒ diverges.
Worked: $\int_1^\infty\frac{\ln x}{x}dx$

Sub $u=\ln x$: $\int\frac{\ln x}{x}dx=\frac{(\ln x)^2}{2}$. So $\int_1^R=\frac{(\ln R)^2}{2}\to\infty$. Diverges.

Worked: $\int_{-\infty}^0 2^x\,dx$

$\int 2^x dx=\frac{2^x}{\ln 2}$. $\int_{-T}^0=\frac{1}{\ln2}-\frac{2^{-T}}{\ln2}\to\frac{1}{\ln2}$ as $T\to\infty$. Converges to $\frac{1}{\ln 2}$.

$\int_0^\infty\cos x\,dx$: $\int_0^R\cos x=\sin R$, which oscillates — limit DNE ⇒ diverges (not "0"!).

⭐ Part B — FTC + chain rule

If $F(x)=\displaystyle\int_{a}^{g(x)} f(t)\,dt$, then $F'(x)=f(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$. (Plug the upper limit into the integrand, multiply by the derivative of that limit.) If BOTH limits depend on $x$, subtract: $\frac{d}{dx}\int_{u(x)}^{v(x)}f=f(v)v'-f(u)u'$.

Worked: $F(x)=\int_1^{e^x}(\ln t)^{15}dt$, find $F'(1)$

$F'(x)=(\ln(e^x))^{15}\cdot\frac{d}{dx}e^x=(x)^{15}\cdot e^x$. At $x=1$: $F'(1)=1^{15}\cdot e^1=\boxed{e}$.

⭐ Part B — Riemann sum ↔ integral

Recognise $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac1n\sum_{k=1}^n f\!\left(\frac kn\right)=\int_0^1 f(x)\,dx$. E.g. $\lim\frac1n\sum(\frac kn)^2=\int_0^1 x^2dx=\frac13$.

Ch 10–15 · Vectors, Matrices, Determinants, Systems

Dot product

$u\cdot v=u_1v_1+u_2v_2(+u_3v_3)=\|u\|\|v\|\cos\theta$.

Orthogonal $\iff u\cdot v=0$.

Cross product (3-space)

$u\times v=\begin{vmatrix}i&j&k\\u_1&u_2&u_3\\v_1&v_2&v_3\end{vmatrix}$.

$\|u\times v\|$ = area of parallelogram. $u\times v\perp$ both.

Worked: $(1,1,0)\times(1,2,2)$

$i(1\cdot2-0\cdot2)-j(1\cdot2-0\cdot1)+k(1\cdot2-1\cdot1)=(2,\,-2,\,1)$.

Determinants (Ch 14)

2×2: $\det\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}=ad-bc$.

3×3: cofactor-expand along the row/column with the most zeros. Signs alternate $\begin{smallmatrix}+&-&+\end{smallmatrix}$.

Properties: $\det(AB)=\det A\det B$ · $\det(A^{-1})=1/\det A$ · $\det(A^T)=\det A$ · $\det(cA)=c^n\det A$ · triangular ⇒ product of diagonal.

⭐ Worked: determinant puzzle — $\det(ABAB)=36$, $1<\det A<\det B$ integers

$\det(ABAB)=(\det A\,\det B)^2=36\Rightarrow\det A\,\det B=6$. With $1<\det A<\det B$ integers ⇒ $\det A=2,\ \det B=3$.

⭐ Worked: $\det(2A)$ when $\det A=5$ ($A$ is 2×2)

$\det(2A)=2^2\det A=4\cdot5=20$. (Common trap: it is NOT $2\cdot5$.)

Inverse (Ch 13)

$A^{-1}=\frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{bmatrix}d&-b\\-c&a\end{bmatrix}$. Exists $\iff ad-bc\ne0$. For larger: augment $[A\,|\,I]$ and row-reduce to $[I\,|\,A^{-1}]$.

⭐ Gaussian elimination & classifying solutions (Ch 12)

Row-reduce the augmented matrix $[A\,|\,b]$. Read off the bottom row:

  • $[0\ 0\ 0\,|\,k]$ with $k\ne0$ ⇒ no solution (inconsistent).
  • $[0\ 0\ 0\,|\,0]$ ⇒ a free variable ⇒ infinitely many solutions.
  • A pivot in every column ⇒ unique solution.
⭐ Worked: $\begin{bmatrix}1&0&2&|&1\\0&1&1&|&1\\0&0&0&|&k\end{bmatrix}$ — for which $k$ a solution? unique?

Last row says $0=k$. So a solution exists only if $k=0$. When $k=0$, column 3 has no pivot ⇒ $x_3$ is free ⇒ infinitely many solutions (not unique).

Null space

Null space of $A$ = all $x$ with $Ax=0$. Row-reduce, set pivots in terms of free variables, write as a span. If $A$ invertible ($\det\ne0$) the null space is just $\{0\}$.

Worked: null space of $\begin{bmatrix}1&0&5\\0&1&1\\0&0&1\end{bmatrix}$

Reduces to identity (3 pivots, $\det=1\ne0$) ⇒ only solution is $x=0$. Null space $=\{(0,0,0)\}$.

Ch 16 · Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors excl 16.1 PageRank

The method (memorise — not on sheet):

  1. Eigenvalues: solve $\det(A-\lambda I)=0$ (the characteristic equation).
  2. Eigenvectors: for each $\lambda$, find the null space of $(A-\lambda I)$ — i.e. solve $(A-\lambda I)v=0$.

2×2 shortcut: $\lambda^2-(\text{trace})\lambda+\det=0$, where trace $=a+d$.

⭐ Worked: full eigen of $A=\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\4&1\end{bmatrix}$

$\det(A-\lambda I)=(1-\lambda)^2-4=0\Rightarrow(1-\lambda)^2=4\Rightarrow 1-\lambda=\pm2\Rightarrow\lambda=-1,\ 3$.

$\lambda=3$: $(A-3I)=\begin{bmatrix}-2&1\\4&-2\end{bmatrix}$ ⇒ $-2x+y=0$ ⇒ $v=(1,2)$.

$\lambda=-1$: $\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\4&2\end{bmatrix}$ ⇒ $2x+y=0$ ⇒ $v=(1,-2)$.

Worked: "Is $(1,-1)$ an eigenvector of $\begin{bmatrix}1&3\\3&1\end{bmatrix}$?"

$A\binom{1}{-1}=\binom{1-3}{3-1}=\binom{-2}{2}=-2\binom{1}{-1}$. Yes — eigenvector with $\lambda=-2$.

Triangular / diagonal matrix ⇒ eigenvalues are the diagonal entries (no algebra needed).
Useful proofs that recur: if $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of invertible $B$ then $\lambda\ne0$ and $1/\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $B^{-1}$. If $A^2=A$ (idempotent), eigenvalues can only be $0$ or $1$ (since $\lambda^2=\lambda$).

Ch 17 · Vector Spaces, Span, Independence excl 17.8–17.11

Linear independence

$\{v_1,\dots,v_k\}$ independent $\iff$ the only solution to $\sum c_i v_i=0$ is all $c_i=0$.

Two vectors: dependent $\iff$ one is a scalar multiple of the other.

Span & basis

Span = all linear combinations. A basis is a linearly independent spanning set. $\dim\mathbb{R}^n=n$.

⭐ Subspace test (the "is this a vector space?" question)

A subset $S$ is a subspace $\iff$ all three hold:

  1. Contains $0$ (the zero vector). ← check this FIRST, it kills most.
  2. Closed under addition: $u,v\in S\Rightarrow u+v\in S$.
  3. Closed under scalar multiplication: $u\in S,\,c\in\mathbb R\Rightarrow cu\in S$.
