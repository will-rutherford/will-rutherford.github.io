Your job today isn't to "understand discrete maths" — it's to recognise "oh, this is the divisibility-proof one / the walk-counting one" and run the recipe. Q1 · Logic 7 marks

Part (a): Is the quantified statement true or false?

the statement has $\forall$ and $\exists$ — especially when the order ($\forall\exists$ vs $\exists\forall$) is the whole point.
The order is everything:
  • $\forall n\,\exists r$ = "for every $n$ there's an $r$" — $r$ may depend on $n$. You're given $n$ first, then you pick $r$. Argue TRUE by giving a formula for $r$ in terms of $n$.
  • $\exists r\,\forall n$ = "one single $r$ works for all $n$ at once." Much stronger. Argue TRUE by naming one fixed $r$; argue FALSE by showing any $r$ fails for some $n$.
To prove a $\forall$ statement false: one counterexample. To prove an $\exists$ true: one witness.
2025 Q1(a) — $\forall n\in\mathbb Z,\exists r\in\mathbb R$ s.t. $n=\lfloor r\rfloor$ is TRUE (given $n$, pick $r=n$). The swapped one $\exists r\,\forall n$ is FALSE (one fixed $r$ can't floor to every integer). The "$\forall\exists$" version is the true one.

Part (b): Prove an argument is valid (cite rules of inference)

you're given numbered premises and a conclusion ($\therefore$). The rules are on your formula sheet — just cite them.
  1. List each premise as a numbered line.
  2. Look for instant combos: an OR + a NOT-of-one-side → Elimination; an implication + its antecedent → Modus Ponens; implication + negated consequent → Modus Tollens; two implications chaining → Transitivity.
  3. Every new line cites the line numbers used and the rule name.
  4. Build toward the conclusion; last line = conclusion.
Worked (2025 Q1b): premises $a\to(b\lor c)$, $b\to d$, ${\sim}b\to{\sim}c$, $a$; prove $d$
1. a → (b ∨ c) premise 2. b → d premise 3. ~b → ~c premise 4. a premise 5. b ∨ c 1,4 Modus Ponens 6. c → b 3, Contrapositive (≡ of premise 3) 7. b ∨ b 5, using c→b on the c case (division into cases) → b [cleaner:] from 5 (b∨c) and 3 (~b→~c): suppose ~b; then ~c (MT-style via 3), contradicting b∨c ⇒ b must hold 8. b division into cases / elimination 9. d 2,8 Modus Ponens ∴ valid
Key insight: premise 3 (${\sim}b\to{\sim}c$) is equivalent to $c\to b$, so either way you land on $b$, then Modus Ponens with premise 2 gives $d$.
Two famous INVALID forms — never use them: affirming the consequent ($p\to q$, $q$, $\therefore p$) and denying the antecedent ($p\to q$, ${\sim}p$, $\therefore{\sim}q$). An uncited line earns nothing.

Q2 · Number Theory 11 marks

(a),(b): Divisibility proofs — "if $n$ odd then $4\mid(n^2-1)$"

"prove $d \mid (\text{expression})$" or "prove ... is divisible by ...". $d\mid a$ means $a=d\cdot k$ for an integer $k$.
  1. Turn the hypothesis into an equation: $n$ odd → write $n=2k+1$ (even → $n=2k$).
  2. Substitute and expand the expression.
  3. Factor out the divisor so it reads $d\times(\text{integer})$.
  4. State the leftover is an integer → divisibility holds by definition.
Worked: $n$ odd ⇒ $4\mid(n^2-1)$, and the stronger $8\mid(n^2-1)$

$n=2k+1$: $n^2-1=(2k+1)^2-1=4k^2+4k=4(k^2+k)=4k(k+1)$.

So $4\mid(n^2-1)$. For 8: $k(k+1)$ is a product of two consecutive integers, so one is even ⇒ $k(k+1)$ is even ⇒ $4k(k+1)$ is divisible by $8$. ∎

Alternative: $n^2-1=(n-1)(n+1)$; $n$ odd ⇒ both factors even ⇒ product divisible by 4 (and one of two consecutive evens is a multiple of 4 ⇒ by 8).

(c): Euclidean algorithm for $\gcd$

"using the Euclidean algorithm, compute $\gcd(a,b)$."
Repeatedly divide: $a=bq+r$. Then shift: old $b$ becomes new $a$, remainder $r$ becomes new $b$. Stop at remainder 0. The last nonzero remainder is the gcd.
Worked: $\gcd(192,75)$
192 = 75·2 + 42 75 = 42·1 + 33 42 = 33·1 + 9 33 = 9·3 + 6 9 = 6·1 + 3 6 = 3·2 + 0 ← stop gcd(192,75) = 3 (last nonzero remainder)

(d): lcm from gcd

"using your answer to (c), compute $\operatorname{lcm}$."
$\gcd(a,b)\times\operatorname{lcm}(a,b)=a\times b$, so $\operatorname{lcm}=\dfrac{a\times b}{\gcd}$.
2025: $\operatorname{lcm}(192,75)=\dfrac{192\times75}{3}=\dfrac{14400}{3}=4800$.
The gcd is the last nonzero remainder, not the 0. Keep every remainder in $0\le r<\text{divisor}$.

Q3 · Induction / Modular Arithmetic 5 marks

"prove for all $n\in\mathbb N$, [expression with a power like $7^n$] $\equiv 0\pmod m$" — i.e. prove $m$ divides it for every $n$.
Induction (safest when there's a $\,7^n$ term):
  1. Base case $n=1$: plug in, show it's $\equiv0\pmod m$.
  2. Hypothesis: assume true for $n=k$, i.e. $\text{expr}(k)=m\cdot M$ for some integer $M$.
  3. Step: write $\text{expr}(k{+}1)$, peel off one factor of the base ($7^{k+1}=7\cdot7^k$), then substitute the hypothesis ($7^k=mM-\dots$) and simplify to $m\times(\text{integer})$.
Worked: $7^n+3n-1\equiv0\pmod 9$ for all $n\in\mathbb N$
Base n=1: 7+3−1 = 9 ≡ 0 (mod 9) ✓ Assume: 7^k + 3k − 1 = 9M ⇒ 7^k = 9M − 3k + 1 Step n=k+1: 7^(k+1) + 3(k+1) − 1 = 7·7^k + 3k + 2 = 7(9M − 3k + 1) + 3k + 2 = 63M − 21k + 7 + 3k + 2 = 63M − 18k + 9 = 9(7M − 2k + 1) ← 9 × integer ✓ So it holds for k+1. By induction, true for all n. ∎
In the step you MUST use the hypothesis to rewrite $7^k$ — don't just restate the goal. The move is always "peel the base factor, substitute, factor out $m$."

Q4 · Functions 10 marks

2025 used $f:\mathbb R\times\mathbb R\to\mathbb Z$, $f(x,y)=\lfloor xy\rfloor$.

Image of a set

"what is the image of $A$?"
Push the set through $f$: compute the outputs, collect them. Track min/max (check turning points, not just endpoints).
2025 (a): image of $[-1,1]\times[-2,2]$. $xy$ ranges over $[-2,2]$, so $\lfloor xy\rfloor\in\{-2,-1,0,1,2\}$.

One-to-one (injective)?

"is $f$ one-to-one?"
Prove: assume $f(a)=f(b)$, derive $a=b$. Disprove: two different inputs, same output.
2025 (b): NOT 1-1. $f(1,2)=\lfloor2\rfloor=2=f(2,1)$ but $(1,2)\neq(2,1)$.

Onto (surjective)?

"is $f$ onto?"
Prove: take arbitrary $y$ in codomain, find an $x$ with $f(x)=y$ (and check $x$ is in the domain). Disprove: name a $y$ nothing maps to.
2025 (c): onto $\mathbb Z$? YES — for any integer $n$, take $(x,y)=(n,1)$: $\lfloor n\cdot1\rfloor=n$.

Preimage size

"is $f^{-1}(v)$ finite / countably infinite / uncountable?"
$f^{-1}(v)=\{x:\ f(x)=v\}$ — find all inputs giving $v$. A whole interval/region of reals ⇒ uncountable.
2025 (d): $f^{-1}(0)=\{(x,y):0\le xy<1\}$ — a 2-D region of reals ⇒ uncountable.
To prove 1-1 you must START from $f(a)=f(b)$ and DERIVE $a=b$ — showing equal inputs give equal outputs proves nothing. "Onto" depends on the codomain stated.

Q5 · Sets & Cardinality 13 marks

(a): Power-set / product counting

"$|\mathcal P(\dots)|$" or "$|A\times B|$".
$|A\times B|=|A|\cdot|B|$, and $|\mathcal P(A)|=2^{|A|}$. Combine: $|\mathcal P(S\times T)|=2^{|S|\cdot|T|}$.
2025 (a): $|S|=3,|T|=2$ ⇒ $|S\times T|=6$ ⇒ $|\mathcal P(S\times T)|=2^6=\mathbf{64}$.

(b): Infinite union

"evaluate $\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty \dots$".
Ask: which elements are in at least one set? Chase a general element; justify by inclusion both ways.
Worked (2025 b): $\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty\{x\in\mathbb N: x\equiv1\pmod{p_i}\}$

$x\equiv1\pmod{p_i}$ means $p_i\mid(x-1)$. The union = all $x\in\mathbb N$ such that some prime divides $x-1$. Every $x\ge2$ has $x-1\ge1$; if $x-1\ge2$ it has a prime factor (so $x$ is included), and $x=2$ gives $x-1=1$... but $x\equiv1\pmod{p_1{=}2}$ means $2\mid(x-1)$, true for all odd $x$. Working it through, the union is all of $\mathbb N$ except where $x-1$ has no prime factor — i.e. except $x=1$ (since $x-1=0$, and $0\equiv1$? no). Net: the union is $\mathbb N\setminus\{1\}$ — every natural $\ge2$. (Justify by: for $x\ge2$, pick any prime $p_i\mid(x-1)$ when $x>2$, and $p_1=2$ catches the odd ones; show $x=1$ is excluded since $1\equiv1\pmod{p_i}$ would need $p_i\mid0$ — true actually, so check the exact set definition carefully on the day.)

(c): Function with uncountable domain/codomain, countable range

"give an example of a function whose domain & codomain are uncountable but range is countably infinite."
Answer: $f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$, $f(x)=\lfloor x\rfloor$. Domain $\mathbb R$ (uncountable), codomain $\mathbb R$ (uncountable), range $=\mathbb Z$ (countably infinite). State domain & codomain explicitly.

(d): Schröder-Bernstein

"use Schröder-Bernstein to prove $|A|=|B|$."
Find an injection $A\to B$ AND an injection $B\to A$. That's it — no bijection needed. (S-B is on your formula sheet.)
Worked (2025 d): $|\mathbb R\cup\{\infty\}|=|\mathbb R|$

$\mathbb R\to\mathbb R\cup\{\infty\}$: identity $g(x)=x$ — injective. ⇒ $|\mathbb R|\le|\mathbb R\cup\{\infty\}|$.

$\mathbb R\cup\{\infty\}\to\mathbb R$: "make room" (Hilbert hotel): send $\infty\mapsto0$; each non-negative integer $n\mapsto n+1$; everything else $\mapsto$ itself. Injective. ⇒ $|\mathbb R\cup\{\infty\}|\le|\mathbb R|$.

Both injections ⇒ by Schröder-Bernstein, $|\mathbb R\cup\{\infty\}|=|\mathbb R|$. ∎

One injection only gives $\le$. You need both directions for equality, and each must be shown injective.

Q6 · Relations 8 marks

2025: $a\,\rho\,b \iff b=a\cdot2^i$ for some integer $i\ge0$ (on $\mathbb N$).

"is $\rho$ reflexive / symmetric / transitive / antisymmetric? Explain." — four mini-questions, 2 marks each.
PropertyTestTo proveTo disprove
Reflexive$a\,\rho\,a$ for all $a$?show the rule holds at $b=a$one $a$ failing
Symmetric$a\rho b\Rightarrow b\rho a$?assume $a\rho b$, derive $b\rho a$one pair: $a\rho b$ but not $b\rho a$
Transitive$a\rho b\ \&\ b\rho c\Rightarrow a\rho c$?assume both, chain them$a,b,c$ breaking it
Antisymmetric$a\rho b\ \&\ b\rho a\Rightarrow a=b$?assume both, derive $a=b$$a\ne b$ with both holding
Worked: the 2025 relation $b=a\cdot2^i$, $i\ge0$

Reflexive ✓: $a=a\cdot2^0$, so take $i=0$. Holds for all $a$.

Symmetric ✗: $1\rho2$ (since $2=1\cdot2^1$) but NOT $2\rho1$ (would need $1=2\cdot2^i$, impossible for $i\ge0$). Counterexample.

Transitive ✓: $b=a2^i$, $c=b2^j$ ⇒ $c=a2^{i+j}$, and $i+j\ge0$. So $a\rho c$.

Antisymmetric ✓: $b=a2^i$ and $a=b2^j$ ⇒ $a=a2^{i+j}$ ⇒ $i+j=0$ ⇒ $i=j=0$ ⇒ $a=b$.

(Reflexive + antisymmetric + transitive ⇒ this is a partial order.)
Antisymmetric is only tested where BOTH $a\rho b$ and $b\rho a$ hold; if that never happens, it's vacuously antisymmetric (still counts as YES). Symmetric and antisymmetric are NOT opposites.

Q7 · Groups 10 marks

The 4 group axioms (your checklist)

C-A-I-I: Closure (stays in set) · Associativity · Identity $e$ exists · Inverses exist (in the set). Abelian = also commutative (bonus, not required).

(a): Cayley table + is it cyclic?

"write out the Cayley table for $(\mathbb Z_5-\{0\},\times)$. Is it cyclic?"
Grid of (row)×(col) mod 5. Then cyclic? = does one element's powers hit everything? Compute powers of a candidate until you return to the identity; if you get all elements, it's a generator ⇒ cyclic.
Worked: Cayley table $\{1,2,3,4\}$ under $\times$ mod 5
× | 1 2 3 4 ---+------------ 1 | 1 2 3 4 2 | 2 4 1 3 3 | 3 1 4 2 4 | 4 3 2 1 Identity = 1. Inverses: 1→1, 2→3, 3→2, 4→4 (all exist) ⇒ group. Cyclic? powers of 2: 2¹=2, 2²=4, 2³=8≡3, 2⁴=16≡1 → {2,4,3,1} = everything. So 2 is a generator ⇒ YES, cyclic. (Generators: 2 and 3.)
A group's Cayley table is a Latin square: every element appears once per row and once per column. Quick sanity check.

(b): Is it a group? / Are two groups isomorphic?

"is $(G-\{0\},\times)$ a group?" or "is $(G,+)$ isomorphic to $(\mathbb Q,+)$?"
Is it a group? Check the 4 axioms; to say NO, find ONE that fails (usually missing inverses — the inverse falls outside the set).
Isomorphic? To prove NO, name a structural property one has and the other lacks (cyclic? abelian? countable? element of order 2? is every element "halvable"/a square?). To prove YES, give a map and check it's operation-preserving + injective + surjective.
Worked (2025 b): $(G-\{0\},\times)$ for $G=\{a+bi:a,b\in\mathbb Z\}$ — a group?

NO. It's closed and associative with identity $1$, but most elements have no inverse in $G$: e.g. $2$'s inverse would be $\tfrac12\notin G$ (Gaussian integers have inverses only for $\pm1,\pm i$). Missing inverses ⇒ not a group.

Worked (2025 b): is $(G,+)\cong(\mathbb Q,+)$?

NO. Use a structural difference. In $(\mathbb Q,+)$ every element is "divisible": for any $q$ and any $n$ there's $x$ with $nx=q$ (namely $q/n$). In $(G,+)=(\mathbb Z[i],+)$ this fails: $1+0i$ cannot be halved within $G$ (no Gaussian integer $x$ with $x+x=1$). A property true in one and false in the other ⇒ not isomorphic. (Also valid: $\mathbb Z[i]\cong\mathbb Z^2$ is "2-generated"/lattice-like, $\mathbb Q$ is not.)

"I can't find a bijection" is NOT a proof of non-isomorphism. You must point to a concrete structural invariant that differs. And the identity isn't always 0 or 1 — solve $x*e=x$ for it.

Q8 · Counting & Graphs 16 marks — the big one

(a),(b): Permutations with adjacency

"arrange ... so that $X$ is adjacent to $Y$" (and maybe "but not adjacent to $Z$").
Adjacent → glue into a block: treat the pair as one object ⇒ $(n{-}1)$ objects ⇒ $(n{-}1)!$, then $\times2$ for the block's internal order ($XY$ or $YX$).
"Not adjacent" → subtract: (total with first constraint) − (those where the forbidden adjacency also happens).
Worked (2025): 6 letters A–F, A adjacent to C; then A adj C but NOT adj B

(a) A adjacent C: glue [AC] → 5 objects → $5!\times2=240$.

(b) A adj C but not adj B: subtract the cases where A is also adjacent to B. If A touches both, A is the middle of a trio B-A-C or C-A-B (2 orders): glue trio → 4 objects → $4!\times2=48$. Answer $=240-48=\mathbf{192}$.

In the "adjacent to both" step, A is the middle — only 2 orders (B-A-C, C-A-B), not $3!$. Forgetting the $\times2$ on a free pair is the other classic slip.

(c): Handshake lemma

"find the degree of the remaining vertex" / "how many edges" / "does such a graph exist?"
Sum of all degrees $=2\times$(number of edges) — always even. Solve for the unknown.
2025 (c): 11 edges, 7 vertices, four of degree 3, two of degree 4. Total degree $=2\times11=22$; known $=4(3)+2(4)=20$; last vertex $=22-20=\mathbf2$.
If a degree sum is odd, no such graph exists. In a simple graph also need max degree $\le n-1$. A loop adds 2 to a degree.

(d),(e): Counting walks of length $k$ — the matrix method

"how many walks from $A$ to $B$ following exactly $k$ edges?" (walks = edges, repeats allowed, can backtrack).
Number of walks of length $k$ from $A$ to $B$ = the $(A,B)$ entry of $M^k$, where $M$ is the adjacency matrix.
Hand method: label each vertex with a running count. Start vertex = 1 (length 0). To get counts after one more step, each vertex's new value = sum of its neighbours' current values. Repeat $k$ times; read off $B$.
Worked (2025 d): $3\times3$ grid of dots, walks of length 4 from corner A to opposite corner B

Label A=1, propagate "new value = sum of neighbours" four times across the grid. The corner B ends at 6. (Equivalently the $(A,B)$ entry of $M^4$ = 6. Because the grid is undirected, walks may go back and forth — this is why the matrix/propagation method is needed, not just monotone lattice paths.)

Worked (2025 e): $5\times5$ grid of dots, walks of length 8 corner to corner

Same propagation, 8 steps: the full $5\times5$ grid gives 70 walks of length 8 from A to B. For part (ii) (the grid with an edge/vertex modified in the figure), rebuild $M$ with that change and recount — same method, different $M$.

Walks allow repeated edges/vertices — do NOT subtract for revisits (that would be a "path" or "trail"). Add only from neighbours actually connected by an edge.
