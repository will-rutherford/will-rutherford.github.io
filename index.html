💧 Statement of Cash Flows highest priority

First: which activity does it go in?

OperatingInvestingFinancing
Day-to-day: customer receipts, payments to suppliers/employees, operating expenses, income taxBuy/sell PPE & investments; interest & dividends RECEIVEDIssue/repay shares & debt; interest & dividends PAID
Interest/dividends received = investing; interest/dividends paid = financing; income tax = operating. Exam questions specify these explicitly — read carefully, it changes the answer.

DIRECT method — operating section

Convert each accrual figure to cash by adjusting for the working-capital change:

  • Cash from customers = Sales − increase in AR (or + decrease in AR)
  • Cash to suppliers = COGS + increase in inventory − increase in AP (i.e. ± inventory, then ∓ AP)
  • Cash for operating expenses = Operating exp − depreciation (non-cash!) + increase in prepaid − increase in accrued
  • Cash for income tax = Tax expense − increase in tax payable

Net cash from operating = receipts − all payments.

Worked (direct): Coastal Retreats — sales 825,000, COGS 570,000, op exp 88,000 (incl dep 20,500), tax 40,000
Receipts from customers = 825,000 − 3,700 (↑AR) = 821,300
Payments to suppliers = −(570,000 + 4,000 ↑inv + 2,300 ↓AP) = (576,300)
Payments for operating expenses = −(88,000 − 20,500 dep) = (67,500)
Payments for income tax = (42,000)
Net cash from operating = 135,500

Note depreciation (20,500) is stripped out — it never appears in the direct method because no cash moves.

INDIRECT method — reconcile profit to cash

Start with operating profit, then:

  1. + non-cash expenses: add depreciation & amortisation.
  2. ± gain/loss on sale of NCA: subtract a gain, add back a loss (it's not operating).
  3. ± working-capital changes (table below).
  4. − tax paid if not already in profit.

Memory rule: asset up = cash down (you tied money up); liability up = cash up (you delayed paying).

The two derivation tricks they always sneak in

Dividends paid (from RE)

Dividends paid = Profit − (closing RE − opening RE).

Profit increases RE; dividends decrease it. So whatever profit didn't stick in RE was paid out.

e.g. profit 160,721, RE moved +4,600 ⇒ dividends = 156,121.

Proceeds from sale of an asset

Proceeds = Carrying amount + gain (or − loss).

Carrying amount = cost − accumulated depreciation.

e.g. carrying 5,300, gain 4,600 ⇒ proceeds = 9,900 (an investing inflow).

📊 Ratios + Commentary your focus

Good news: the formulae are printed on the exam. You don't have to memorise them — you have to (1) pick the right numbers, (2) compute to the asked decimals/units, and (3) write 2–3 sentences on why it changed. The written part is where students drop easy marks. The full list with meanings is on the ratio sheet.

The number-picking traps

"Average" means $(\text{opening} + \text{closing})/2$ — used in turnover ratios (receivables, inventory, asset), ROA, ROE, and the cash-debt-coverage ratios.
Net credit sales = sales − sales returns & allowances. Use this (not total sales) for receivables turnover and average collection period.
Capital expenditure when not given = closing PPE − opening PPE + depreciation. Needed for free cash flow & the capex ratio.

How to write the commentary (template for full marks)

  1. State the direction: "The current ratio improved from 2.75 to 3.73."
  2. Interpret it: "indicating stronger short-term liquidity — the company can comfortably cover current liabilities."
  3. Give a plausible reason / caveat: "However a ratio this high may signal excess idle cash or slow-moving inventory, i.e. inefficient working-capital management."
Stock phrases that score (memorise a few)
  • Days in inventory ↑ = slower-moving / possibly obsolete stock, overstocking, or a shift in product mix.
  • Days in inventory ↓ = better inventory management, promotions clearing stock, JIT, less obsolescence.
  • Collection period ↑ = lax credit policy, weaker collections, customer disputes, slower-paying customers.
  • Quick ratio < 1 = a liquidity concern; reliant on selling inventory to meet short-term debts.
  • Current ratio very high = strong liquidity but possibly inefficient use of working capital.
  • Times interest earned < ~2–3 = solvency concern; may struggle to service interest.

🏭 Depreciation & Disposal (Q1 every paper)

The 3 methods

  • Straight-line = (cost − residual) / useful life. Same each full year.
  • Diminishing-balance = carrying amount × rate. Ignores residual; carrying amount = cost − accumulated dep so far.
  • Units-of-production = (cost − residual) × (units this period / total units).

First (part) year: multiply by months/12 (for SL and diminishing). e.g. bought 1 Sep, Dec year-end ⇒ ×4/12.

GST: if the cost is given "including 10% GST", divide by 1.1 first to get the GST-exclusive cost. All depreciation uses the ex-GST figure.
Worked: machine $207,000 (ex-GST), residual $25,900, 8-yr, 30,000 hrs, bought 1 Sep, Dec year-end
Straight-line: (207,000 − 25,900)/8 = 22,638/yr.
  Year 1 = 22,638 × 4/12 = 7,546; Year 2 = 22,638.

Diminishing @25%: Year 1 = 207,000 × 25% × 4/12 = 17,250;
  Year 2 = (207,000 − 17,250) × 25% = 47,438.

Units (1,750 hrs): (207,000 − 25,900) × 1,750/30,000 = 10,564.

Disposal gain/loss

  1. Update depreciation to the disposal date.
  2. Carrying amount = cost − accumulated depreciation.
  3. Gain/loss = proceeds − carrying amount (positive = gain, negative = loss).
Dr Cash (proceeds)
Dr Accumulated depreciation
Dr Loss on disposal  (if any)
    Cr Asset (original cost)
    Cr Gain on disposal  (if any)
Worked: truck cost $24,750 incl 10% GST, SL 5-yr, residual $2,250, scrapped (proceeds $0)

Ex-GST cost = 24,750/1.1 = 22,500. Depreciable = 22,500 − 2,250 = 20,250. SL = 4,050/yr × 5 yrs = 20,250 accumulated. Carrying amount = 22,500 − 20,250 = 2,250. Proceeds 0 − 2,250 = Loss $2,250.

🏦 Bank Reconciliation + Allowance for Doubtful Debts

Bank reconciliation (two columns)

BANK STATEMENT sideCOMPANY BOOKS side
Balance per bank statement

− unpresented (outstanding) cheques		Balance per books (cash at bank)
+ interest/notes collected by bank, error corrections that increase cash

Both sides must reconcile to the same adjusted figure.

Adjusting journal entries are made ONLY for the BOOKS-side items (interest, charges, dishonoured cheques, errors). Never for outstanding deposits / unpresented cheques — those are timing differences the bank will catch up on.

Worked: books $3,551; +interest 17; −charges 35; recording error +27; −dishonoured 260; outstanding deposits 450; bank stmt 2,850
Bank side: 2,850 + 450 = 3,300
Books side: 3,551 + 17 + 27 − 260 − 35 = 3,300 ✓

Allowance for doubtful debts

  1. Write-off during the period: Dr Allowance for doubtful debts / Cr Accounts receivable.
  2. Required allowance = % × accounts receivable (closing).
  3. Balance after write-offs = opening allowance − write-offs.
  4. Top-up (the bad debts expense) = required allowance − balance after write-offs.
    Dr Bad debts expense / Cr Allowance for doubtful debts.
Worked: opening allowance 8,650 Cr; write off 3,500; AR 524,000; bring to 2.0%

Write-off: Dr Allowance 3,500 / Cr AR 3,500. Required = 2% × 524,000 = 10,480. Balance after = 8,650 − 3,500 = 5,150 Cr. Top-up = 10,480 − 5,150 = 5,330: Dr Bad debts expense 5,330 / Cr Allowance 5,330.

📒 The Journal-Entry Families

Watch the instruction "narrations required" — if it says so, write a one-line description under each entry or you lose marks.
Warranty provision

Claims incurred: Dr Warranty provision / Cr Wages payable (or Cash/Inventory).
Year-end adjustment: required = % × revenue; balance after claims = opening − claims (may go to a debit!); top-up = required + debit balance (or required − credit balance).

e.g. opening 74,690 Cr, claims 77,500 ⇒ balance = 2,810 debit. Required = 2% × 3,830,000 = 76,600. Adjustment = 76,600 + 2,810 = 79,410: Dr Warranty expense 79,410 / Cr Warranty provision 79,410.

Share issue — application & allotment (via Cash Trust)
On application money received: Dr Cash Trust / Cr Application
On allotment (shares issued): Dr Application / Cr Share capital
  and Dr Cash / Cr Cash Trust (transfer out of trust)
Dr Allotment receivable / Cr Share capital (for the allotment instalment)

Simple fully-paid-on-application issue: Dr Cash / Cr Share capital (shares × price applied).

Dividends — declare then pay
Declaration: Dr Retained earnings (Dividends declared) / Cr Dividends payable
Payment: Dr Dividends payable / Cr Cash

Amount = shares on issue × dividend per share.

Debenture / note redemption (gain or loss)

Redeemed above face (e.g. at 102) ⇒ a loss: Dr Debentures payable (face), Dr Loss on redemption / Cr Cash.
Redeemed below face (e.g. at 98) ⇒ a gain: Dr Debentures payable (face) / Cr Gain on redemption, Cr Cash.

Notes payable + accrued interest

Issue: Dr Cash / Cr Notes payable. Accrue interest at period-end: Dr Interest expense / Cr Interest payable (= principal × rate × months/12). At maturity: Dr Notes payable, Dr Interest payable, Dr Interest expense / Cr Cash.

Revenue received in advance (unearned)

On receipt: Dr Cash / Cr Unearned revenue (revenue received in advance). As earned: Dr Unearned revenue / Cr Revenue.

e.g. 5,000 season tickets × $550 = 2,750,000 unearned over 11 matches ⇒ per match 250,000; after 2 matches: Dr Unearned revenue 500,000 / Cr Revenue 500,000.
