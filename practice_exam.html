Part A — 15 questions × 2 marks (30)

Click any question to reveal its solution.

2A1. Determine whether $\sum_{n=1}^\infty\left(\frac1n-\frac1{n+2}\right)$ converges.
Telescoping. Partial sum $S_N=\left(1+\frac12\right)-\frac1{N+1}-\frac1{N+2}\to\frac32$. Converges (to $3/2$).
2A2. Determine whether $\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{3n+1}{n}$ converges.
$a_n=3+\frac1n\to3\ne0$. By the nth-term (divergence) test, diverges.
2A3. Determine whether $\sum_{n=1}^\infty\left(\frac34\right)^n$ converges.
Geometric, $r=\frac34$, $|r|<1$ ⇒ converges (to $\frac{3/4}{1-3/4}=3$).
2A4. Use the comparison test to show $\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{1}{n^2+5}$ converges.
$0<\frac{1}{n^2+5}\le\frac1{n^2}$, and $\sum\frac1{n^2}$ converges ($p=2>1$). By comparison, converges.
2A5. Evaluate $\displaystyle\int x e^{x}\,dx$.
By parts $u=x,\,dv=e^x dx$: $xe^x-\int e^x dx=(x-1)e^x+C$.
2A6. Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \frac{2x}{x^2+3}\,dx$.
Form $\frac{f'}{f}$ with $f=x^2+3$: $=\ln(x^2+3)+C$.
2A7. Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{x(x+1)}\,dx$.
Partial fractions $\frac1x-\frac1{x+1}$: $=\ln|x|-\ln|x+1|+C=\ln\left|\frac{x}{x+1}\right|+C$.
2A8. Calculate the determinant of $\begin{bmatrix}2&0&1\\1&3&0\\0&-1&4\end{bmatrix}$.
Expand row 1: $2(3\cdot4-0\cdot(-1))-0+1(1\cdot(-1)-3\cdot0)=2(12)+(-1)=23$.
2A9. Is $\begin{bmatrix}2&4\\1&2\end{bmatrix}$ invertible?
$\det=2\cdot2-4\cdot1=0$ ⇒ not invertible (singular).
2A10. Determine the eigenvalues of $\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\4&1\end{bmatrix}$.
$(1-\lambda)^2-4=0\Rightarrow1-\lambda=\pm2\Rightarrow\lambda=3,\,-1$.
2A11. Compute the dot product $(2,1,-3)\cdot(1,4,2)$ and state whether the vectors are orthogonal.
$2+4-6=0$. Since the dot product is $0$, they ARE orthogonal.
2A12. Compute the cross product $(1,1,0)\times(1,2,2)$.
$i(1\cdot2-0\cdot2)-j(1\cdot2-0\cdot1)+k(1\cdot2-1\cdot1)=(2,-2,1)$.
2A13. Is $\binom{1}{-1}$ an eigenvector of $\begin{bmatrix}1&3\\3&1\end{bmatrix}$? If so give its eigenvalue.
$A\binom1{-1}=\binom{1-3}{3-1}=\binom{-2}{2}=-2\binom1{-1}$. Yes, eigenvalue $\lambda=-2$.
2A14. Are $\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\1\end{pmatrix}$ and $\begin{pmatrix}2\\4\\3\end{pmatrix}$ linearly independent?
If dependent, the second $=c\times$ first: $c=2$ from first two entries gives $(2,4,2)\ne(2,4,3)$. Not a scalar multiple ⇒ linearly independent.
2A15. Determine $\frac{d}{dx}\displaystyle\int_1^{1+x}\ln t\,dt$.
FTC + chain: $\ln(1+x)\cdot\frac{d}{dx}(1+x)=\ln(1+x)$.

Part B — long questions (70)

15B1. (a) Find the Taylor series for $f(x)=e^x$ about $x=1$ and determine its radius of convergence. (b) Use part (a) to find the Taylor series for $g(x)=(x-1)e^x$ about $x=1$.
(a) $f^{(n)}(1)=e$ for all $n$ ⇒ $e^x=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{e}{n!}(x-1)^n$. Ratio $=\frac{|x-1|}{n+1}\to0<1$ for all $x$ ⇒ $R=\infty$.
(b) $g(x)=(x-1)\sum\frac{e}{n!}(x-1)^n=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{e}{n!}(x-1)^{n+1}$.
8B2. Find the radius and interval of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{2^n}{n\,3^n}(x-1)^n$.
Ratio $\to\frac23|x-1|<1\Rightarrow R=\frac32$, interval $(-\frac12,\frac52)$ before endpoints. At $x=\frac52$: $\sum\frac1n$ diverges. At $x=-\frac12$: $\sum\frac{(-1)^n}{n}$ converges. Interval: $[-\frac12,\frac52)$.
10B3. For each improper integral, determine convergence and evaluate if convergent. (a) $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^0 2^x dx$ (b) $\displaystyle\int_1^\infty\frac{\ln x}{x}dx$.
(a) $\int 2^x=\frac{2^x}{\ln2}$; $\int_{-T}^0=\frac{1-2^{-T}}{\ln2}\to\frac1{\ln2}$. Converges to $1/\ln2$.
(b) $u=\ln x$: antiderivative $\frac{(\ln x)^2}{2}$; $\int_1^R=\frac{(\ln R)^2}{2}\to\infty$. Diverges.
10B4. $F(x)=\displaystyle\int_1^{e^{x}}(\ln t)^{3}\,dt$. (a) Find $F'(x)$ without evaluating the integral. (b) Find $F'(0)$.
(a) FTC+chain: $F'(x)=(\ln e^x)^3\cdot e^x=x^3 e^x$. (b) $F'(0)=0$.
12B5. True/False — prove or give a counter-example (3 marks each). (a) The set $\{(a,b)\in\mathbb R^2 : a-b=1\}$ with standard operations is a vector space. (b) For any 2×2 matrix $B$, $A=\begin{bmatrix}3&0\\0&3\end{bmatrix}$ satisfies $AB=BA$. (c) If $u,v$ are linearly independent then they are orthogonal. (d) If $A$ is $3\times3$ then $\det(3A)=3\det A$.
(a) FALSE — no zero vector ($0-0=0\ne1$); not closed.
(b) TRUE — $A=3I$, a scalar multiple of the identity, commutes with every matrix.
(c) FALSE — $(1,0),(1,1)$ are independent but $(1,0)\cdot(1,1)=1\ne0$.
(d) FALSE — $\det(3A)=3^3\det A=27\det A$.
8B6. The augmented matrix for $Ax=b$ reduces to $\begin{bmatrix}1&0&2&|&1\\0&1&1&|&1\\0&0&0&|&k\end{bmatrix}$. For which $k$ does a solution exist? Is it unique? Also find the null space of $A$ when a solution exists.
Solution exists $\iff k=0$ (else $0=k$ is impossible). Then $x_3$ is free ⇒ not unique (infinitely many). Null space: set RHS $=0$: $x_1=-2x_3,\,x_2=-x_3$ ⇒ span$\{(-2,-1,1)\}$.
7B7. Matrices $A,B$ are square with integer determinants, $1<\det A<\det B$, and $\det(ABAB)=36$. Find $\det A$ and $\det B$.
$\det(ABAB)=(\det A\det B)^2=36\Rightarrow\det A\det B=6$. Integers with $1<\det A<\det B$ ⇒ $\det A=2,\det B=3$.
8B8. Determine the eigenvalues and corresponding eigenvectors of $A=\begin{bmatrix}1&6\\5&2\end{bmatrix}$.
$\det(A-\lambda I)=(1-\lambda)(2-\lambda)-30=\lambda^2-3\lambda-28=(\lambda-7)(\lambda+4)=0\Rightarrow\lambda=7,-4$.
$\lambda=7$: $\begin{bmatrix}-6&6\\5&-5\end{bmatrix}\Rightarrow x=y\Rightarrow(1,1)$.
$\lambda=-4$: $\begin{bmatrix}5&6\\5&6\end{bmatrix}\Rightarrow5x+6y=0\Rightarrow(6,-5)$.
2B9. Is the span of $(1,1)$ a subspace of $\mathbb R^2$?
Yes — it contains $0$ (take scalar $0$), and is closed under addition and scalar multiplication (any combination of $(1,1)$ is again a multiple of $(1,1)$). It's the line $y=x$, a 1-dimensional subspace.
