Q1 ‚Äî Logic (7)

21(a) Which is true: (i) $\forall n\in\mathbb Z,\exists r\in\mathbb R$ s.t. $n=\lfloor r\rfloor$, or (ii) $\exists r\in\mathbb R$ s.t. $\forall n\in\mathbb Z,\ n=\lfloor r\rfloor$?
(i) is true; (ii) is false.
(i) $\forall n\,\exists r$: given any integer $n$, choose $r=n$ (or any $r\in[n,n+1)$); then $\lfloor r\rfloor=n$. The $r$ is allowed to depend on $n$, so this works. ‚úì
(ii) $\exists r\,\forall n$: this claims ONE fixed $r$ floors to every integer simultaneously ‚Äî impossible, since $\lfloor r\rfloor$ is a single number. ‚úó
51(b) Prove valid: $a\to(b\lor c)$, $\ b\to d$, $\ {\sim}b\to{\sim}c$, $\ a$, $\ \therefore d$.
1. a ‚Üí (b ‚à® c) premise 2. b ‚Üí d premise 3. ~b ‚Üí ~c premise 4. a premise 5. b ‚à® c 1, 4 Modus Ponens 6. c ‚Üí b 3, Contrapositive (~b‚Üí~c is equivalent to c‚Üíb) 7. b 5, 6 Division into cases: if b, done; if c then by (6) b. Either way b. 8. d 2, 7 Modus Ponens ‚à¥ the argument is valid. ‚àé
The pivot is realising premise 3 (${\sim}b\to{\sim}c$) is the contrapositive of $c\to b$. Then both branches of $b\lor c$ lead to $b$.

Q2 ‚Äî Number Theory (11)

32(a) Prove: for all $n\in\mathbb Z$, if $n$ is odd then $4\mid(n^2-1)$.
Let $n$ be odd, so $n=2k+1$ for some integer $k$.
$n^2-1=(2k+1)^2-1=4k^2+4k=4(k^2+k)$.
Since $k^2+k\in\mathbb Z$, we have $n^2-1=4\times(\text{integer})$, so $4\mid(n^2-1)$. ‚àé
32(b) Prove: for all $n\in\mathbb Z$, if $n$ is odd then $8\mid(n^2-1)$.
From (a), $n^2-1=4(k^2+k)=4k(k+1)$.
$k$ and $k+1$ are consecutive integers, so one of them is even ‚áí $k(k+1)$ is even ‚áí $k(k+1)=2m$ for some integer $m$.
Then $n^2-1=4\cdot2m=8m$, so $8\mid(n^2-1)$. ‚àé
The whole trick is "two consecutive integers ‚áí their product is even," giving the extra factor of 2.
32(c) Using the Euclidean algorithm, compute $\gcd(192,75)$.
192 = 75¬∑2 + 42 75 = 42¬∑1 + 33 42 = 33¬∑1 + 9 33 = 9¬∑3 + 6 9 = 6¬∑1 + 3 6 = 3¬∑2 + 0 ‚Üê remainder 0, stop gcd(192, 75) = 3 (last nonzero remainder)
22(d) Using (c), compute $\operatorname{lcm}(192,75)$.
$\operatorname{lcm}(a,b)=\dfrac{a\cdot b}{\gcd(a,b)}=\dfrac{192\times75}{3}=\dfrac{14400}{3}=\mathbf{4800}.$

Q4 ‚Äî Functions: $f:\mathbb R\times\mathbb R\to\mathbb Z,\ f(x,y)=\lfloor xy\rfloor$ (10)

24(a) Image of $[-1,1]\times[-2,2]$ under $f$.
For $x\in[-1,1],y\in[-2,2]$, the product $xy$ ranges over $[-2,2]$ (max $=1\cdot2=2$, min $=1\cdot(-2)=-2$).
Flooring values across $[-2,2]$ gives every integer from $-2$ up to $2$.
Image $=\{-2,-1,0,1,2\}$.
34(b) Is $f$ one-to-one?
No. Counterexample: $f(1,2)=\lfloor2\rfloor=2$ and $f(2,1)=\lfloor2\rfloor=2$, but $(1,2)\neq(2,1)$. Two different inputs, same output ‚áí not injective.
34(c) Is $f$ onto?
Yes. Take any integer $n\in\mathbb Z$ (the codomain). Choose $(x,y)=(n,1)$. Then $f(n,1)=\lfloor n\cdot1\rfloor=\lfloor n\rfloor=n$. So every integer is hit ‚áí onto.
24(d) Is the preimage $f^{-1}(0)$ finite, countably infinite, or uncountable?
Uncountable. $f^{-1}(0)=\{(x,y):\lfloor xy\rfloor=0\}=\{(x,y):0\le xy<1\}$ ‚Äî a 2-dimensional region of the real plane, which contains uncountably many points (e.g. all $(x,0)$ for $x\in\mathbb R$ already gives an uncountable set).

Q5 ‚Äî Sets & Cardinality (13)

35(a) $|S|=3,|T|=2$. What is $|\mathcal P(S\times T)|$?
$|S\times T|=|S|\cdot|T|=3\times2=6$. Then $|\mathcal P(S\times T)|=2^{|S\times T|}=2^6=\mathbf{64}.$
45(b) $p_i=i$th prime. Evaluate $\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty\{x\in\mathbb N: x\equiv1\pmod{p_i}\}$.
$x\equiv1\pmod{p_i}$ means $p_i\mid(x-1)$. The union is all $x\in\mathbb N$ for which some prime $p_i$ divides $x-1$.
‚Ä¢ $x=1$: $x-1=0$, and every prime divides $0$, so $1\equiv1\pmod{p_i}$ for all $i$ ‚Äî so $x=1$ IS in every set, hence in the union.
‚Ä¢ $x=2$: $x-1=1$; no prime divides $1$, so $2$ is in none of the sets ‚áí $2\notin$ union.
‚Ä¢ $x\ge3$: $x-1\ge2$ has a prime factor $p_i$, so $x$ is in that set ‚áí in the union.
Union $=\mathbb N\setminus\{2\}=\{1,3,4,5,6,\dots\}.$
Justify by the two inclusions: every $x\neq2$ is shown to lie in some set; $x=2$ lies in none because $x-1=1$ has no prime factor. (Double-check the $x=1$ edge case under your course's convention for "divides 0" ‚Äî standard convention: every integer divides 0, so $1$ is included.)
25(c) Give a function with uncountable domain & codomain but countably infinite range.
$f:\mathbb R\to\mathbb R$, $f(x)=\lfloor x\rfloor$. Domain $\mathbb R$ (uncountable), codomain $\mathbb R$ (uncountable), range $=\mathbb Z$ (countably infinite). ‚úì
45(d) Use Schr√∂der-Bernstein to prove $|\mathbb R\cup\{\infty\}|=|\mathbb R|$.
Injection $\mathbb R\to\mathbb R\cup\{\infty\}$: $g(x)=x$ (identity). Injective since $\mathbb R\subseteq\mathbb R\cup\{\infty\}$. So $|\mathbb R|\le|\mathbb R\cup\{\infty\}|$.

Injection $\mathbb R\cup\{\infty\}\to\mathbb R$: "shift to make room for $\infty$":
h(‚àû) = 0 h(n) = n+1 if n ‚àà {0,1,2,3,...} (non-negative integers) h(x) = x otherwise
This is injective: $\infty$ maps to $0$; the non-negative integers shift up to $1,2,3,\dots$ (vacating $0$ so it doesn't clash); every other real maps to itself. No two inputs collide. So $|\mathbb R\cup\{\infty\}|\le|\mathbb R|$.

Both injections exist ‚áí by Schr√∂der-Bernstein, $|\mathbb R\cup\{\infty\}|=|\mathbb R|$. ‚àé

Q6 ‚Äî Relations: $a\,\rho\,b\iff b=a\cdot2^i,\ i\ge0$ integer, on $\mathbb N$ (8)

26(a) Reflexive?
Yes. For any $a$, take $i=0$: $a=a\cdot2^0=a$, so $a\,\rho\,a$ holds for every $a\in\mathbb N$.
26(b) Symmetric?
No. Counterexample: $1\,\rho\,2$ since $2=1\cdot2^1$. But $2\,\rho\,1$ would need $1=2\cdot2^i$, i.e. $2^i=\tfrac12$, impossible for integer $i\ge0$. So $a\rho b$ doesn't imply $b\rho a$.
26(c) Transitive?
Yes. Suppose $a\rho b$ and $b\rho c$: $b=a\cdot2^i$ and $c=b\cdot2^j$ with $i,j\ge0$. Then $c=a\cdot2^{i+j}$, and $i+j\ge0$, so $a\,\rho\,c$.
26(d) Antisymmetric?
Yes. Suppose $a\rho b$ and $b\rho a$: $b=a\cdot2^i$ and $a=b\cdot2^j$, $i,j\ge0$. Substituting, $a=a\cdot2^{i+j}$ ‚áí $2^{i+j}=1$ ‚áí $i+j=0$ ‚áí $i=j=0$ ‚áí $b=a$.
Reflexive + antisymmetric + transitive ‚áí $\rho$ is a partial order on $\mathbb N$.

Q7 ‚Äî Groups (10)

47(a) Cayley table for $(\mathbb Z_5-\{0\},\times)$. Is it cyclic?
√ó | 1 2 3 4 ---+------------ 1 | 1 2 3 4 2 | 2 4 1 3 (2¬∑3=6‚â°1, 2¬∑4=8‚â°3) 3 | 3 1 4 2 (3¬∑3=9‚â°4, 3¬∑4=12‚â°2) 4 | 4 3 2 1 (4¬∑4=16‚â°1)
Identity $=1$; every row/column is a permutation of $\{1,2,3,4\}$ (Latin square) and every element has an inverse ($2^{-1}=3,\ 4^{-1}=4$) ‚áí it is a group.
Cyclic? YES. Powers of $2$: $2^1=2,\ 2^2=4,\ 2^3=8\equiv3,\ 2^4=16\equiv1$ ‚Äî these are $\{2,4,3,1\}$ = the whole group. So $2$ is a generator. (So is $3$.)
37(b)(i) $G=\{a+bi:a,b\in\mathbb Z\}$. Is $(G-\{0\},\times)$ a group?
No. It is closed and associative with identity $1$, but most elements lack an inverse in $G$. E.g. $2\in G$ would need $2^{-1}=\tfrac12$, which is not a Gaussian integer. (Only $\pm1,\pm i$ have inverses in $G$.) A failed axiom (inverses) ‚áí not a group.
37(b)(ii) Is $(G,+)$ isomorphic to $(\mathbb Q,+)$?
No. Use a structural property that differs. In $(\mathbb Q,+)$, every element is "divisible by $2$": for any $q$ there is $x\in\mathbb Q$ with $x+x=q$ (take $x=q/2$). In $(G,+)=(\mathbb Z[i],+)$ this fails: $1\in G$ has no $x\in G$ with $x+x=1$ (would need $x=\tfrac12\notin G$). A property true in one group and false in the other ‚áí they cannot be isomorphic.
Equally valid: $(G,+)\cong(\mathbb Z^2,+)$ is finitely generated (by $1$ and $i$), whereas $(\mathbb Q,+)$ is not finitely generated. Any one clean structural difference earns the marks.

Q8 ‚Äî Counting & Graphs (16)

28(a) Permutations of $A,B,C,D,E,F$ with $A$ adjacent to $C$.
Glue $\{A,C\}$ into one block ‚áí $5$ objects: $[AC],B,D,E,F$. Arrange: $5!=120$. The block has $2$ internal orders ($AC$ or $CA$): $\times2$.
$5!\times2=240$.
38(b) Permutations with $A$ adjacent to $C$ but $A$ not adjacent to $B$.
Use subtraction: (adj to $C$) ‚àí (adj to $C$ AND adj to $B$).
‚Ä¢ adj to $C$ $=240$ (from (a)).
‚Ä¢ adj to $C$ AND adj to $B$: $A$ touches both, so $A$ is the middle of a 3-block: $B\text-A\text-C$ or $C\text-A\text-B$ (2 orders). Glue trio ‚áí $4$ objects ‚áí $4!=24$, $\times2=48$.
$240-48=192$.
38(c) 11 edges, 7 vertices; four vertices degree 3, two degree 4. Degree of the last vertex?
Handshake lemma: sum of degrees $=2\times\text{edges}=2\times11=22$.
Known degrees $=4(3)+2(4)=12+8=20$.
Last vertex $=22-20=\mathbf2$. (Proof = the handshake lemma, which holds because each edge contributes 2 to the degree total.)
28(d) $3\times3$ grid of dots; number of walks of length 4 from corner $A$ to opposite corner $B$.
6 walks.
Method: walks of length $k$ from $A$ to $B$ = the $(A,B)$ entry of $M^4$ ($M$ = adjacency matrix of the grid). Equivalently, label $A=1$ and propagate "new value = sum of neighbours' values" four steps; the corner $B$ ends with $6$.
Intuition: on a 3√ó3 grid the corner-to-corner distance is 4, so length-4 walks are exactly the shortest (monotone) paths: choose which 2 of the 4 steps go "down" = C(4,2)=6.
68(e) Walks of length 8 from $A$ to $B$ on (i) the full $5\times5$ grid, (ii) the modified grid in the figure.
(i) 70 walks. On the $5\times5$ grid the corner-to-corner distance is 8, so length-8 walks are the monotone shortest paths: choose which 4 of 8 steps go down = $\binom{8}{4}=70$. (Same as the $(A,B)$ entry of $M^8$.)

(ii) The figure removes an edge/vertex, which kills some of those 70 paths. Count via: (full 70) ‚àí (monotone paths using the removed edge). Read the exact removed edge off the figure on the day and subtract the paths through it.
